3D Printing Materials Comparison: Which One Should You Choose?

August 15, 2023
Vishal Patra

When diving into the world of 3D printing, one of the most critical decisions is selecting the right material for your project. Each material has unique properties, advantages, and limitations that make it suitable for specific applications. In this comprehensive guide, we'll compare the most common 3D printing materials to help you make an informed decision for your next project.

PLA (Polylactic Acid)

PLA is the most popular material for hobbyist 3D printing, and for good reason.

Properties:

  • Printing Temperature: 180-220°C
  • Bed Temperature: 20-60°C (heated bed optional)
  • Shrinkage: Minimal
  • Biodegradable: Yes

Advantages:

  • Easy to print with minimal warping
  • Doesn't require a heated bed
  • Environmentally friendly (made from renewable resources)
  • Good layer adhesion
  • Available in many colors and finishes
  • Pleasant sweet smell during printing

Disadvantages:

  • Low heat resistance (softens around 60°C)
  • Brittle and can break under stress
  • Not suitable for outdoor use (degrades with UV exposure)
  • Absorbs moisture from the air (needs proper storage)

Best Applications:

  • Prototyping
  • Decorative items
  • Toys and figurines
  • Indoor use items
  • Low-stress applications

ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)

ABS is a petroleum-based plastic widely used in engineering and mechanical applications.

Properties:

  • Printing Temperature: 220-250°C
  • Bed Temperature: 90-110°C
  • Shrinkage: Significant
  • Biodegradable: No

Advantages:

  • Excellent durability and impact resistance
  • Higher temperature resistance (up to 100°C)
  • Flexible and less brittle than PLA
  • Can be acetone smoothed for a professional finish
  • Suitable for mechanical parts

Disadvantages:

  • Harder to print (prone to warping and layer separation)
  • Requires a heated bed and preferably an enclosed printer
  • Emits fumes during printing (requires good ventilation)
  • Not environmentally friendly

Best Applications:

  • Functional parts that need to withstand stress
  • Automotive components
  • Tool handles
  • Electrical enclosures
  • Outdoor applications

PETG (Polyethylene Terephthalate Glycol)

PETG is often considered the middle ground between PLA and ABS, offering a good balance of properties.

Properties:

  • Printing Temperature: 220-250°C
  • Bed Temperature: 70-80°C
  • Shrinkage: Minimal
  • Biodegradable: No, but recyclable

Advantages:

  • Strong and durable
  • Good layer adhesion
  • Better temperature resistance than PLA (up to 80°C)
  • Flexible and impact resistant
  • Chemical resistant
  • Less prone to warping than ABS
  • Food safe (in its pure form)

Disadvantages:

  • Can be stringy during printing
  • Prone to scratches
  • Absorbs moisture (requires proper storage)
  • More expensive than PLA

Best Applications:

  • Functional parts requiring durability
  • Water-tight containers
  • Food containers
  • Mechanical parts with moving components
  • Indoor and outdoor applications

TPU (Thermoplastic Polyurethane)

TPU is a flexible filament that brings elasticity to 3D printing.

Properties:

  • Printing Temperature: 210-230°C
  • Bed Temperature: 30-60°C
  • Shrinkage: Minimal
  • Biodegradable: No

Advantages:

  • Highly flexible and elastic
  • Excellent wear and abrasion resistance
  • Good chemical resistance
  • Shock absorbing
  • Good layer adhesion

Disadvantages:

  • Difficult to print (requires direct drive extruder)
  • Slow printing speeds
  • Can be difficult to get precise dimensions
  • More expensive than rigid filaments

Best Applications:

  • Phone cases
  • Protective covers
  • Flexible joints
  • Gaskets and seals
  • Shoe soles
  • Grips for tools

Resin (SLA/DLP Printing)

Resin is used in SLA/DLP printers rather than FDM printers and offers exceptional detail.

Properties:

  • Curing: UV light
  • Post-processing: Required (washing and additional curing)
  • Shrinkage: Varies by type
  • Biodegradable: No

Advantages:

  • Extremely high detail and resolution
  • Smooth surface finish
  • Various specialized formulations (tough, flexible, castable)
  • Excellent for small, intricate parts

Disadvantages:

  • Messy and requires careful handling
  • Requires post-processing (washing and additional curing)
  • More expensive than filament
  • Toxic before curing (requires gloves, ventilation)
  • Limited build volume compared to FDM
  • More brittle than some filaments

Best Applications:

  • Detailed miniatures and figurines
  • Jewelry prototypes
  • Dental and medical models
  • Highly detailed prototypes
  • Master patterns for molding

Choosing the Right Material

When selecting a material for your 3D printing project, consider these factors:

  1. Mechanical Requirements: What stresses will the part be subjected to? Does it need to be flexible, rigid, or impact-resistant?
  2. Environmental Conditions: Will the part be used outdoors or exposed to heat, UV light, or chemicals?
  3. Aesthetic Requirements: How important is surface finish and detail? Do you need specific colors or transparency?
  4. Printer Capabilities: Can your printer handle the required temperatures and does it have the necessary features (like an enclosed chamber for ABS)?
  5. Budget: Some materials are significantly more expensive than others.

Conclusion

There's no "one-size-fits-all" material in 3D printing. Each has its strengths and weaknesses, making them suitable for different applications. For beginners, PLA remains the easiest starting point. As you gain experience, experimenting with other materials will expand your capabilities and the range of projects you can successfully complete.

Remember that proper storage of your filaments (in airtight containers with desiccants) is crucial for maintaining their print quality, regardless of which material you choose.
