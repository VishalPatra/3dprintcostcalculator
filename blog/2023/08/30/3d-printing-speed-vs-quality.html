3D Printing Speed vs Quality: Finding the Perfect Balance

August 30, 2023
Vishal Patra

In the world of 3D printing, one of the most common dilemmas is finding the balance between print speed and quality. Fast prints save time and increase productivity, but they can result in visible defects. High-quality prints look fantastic but may take significantly longer to complete. So how do you optimize your printer settings to get the best of both worlds? In this guide, we'll explore the key factors that influence print speed and quality, and provide practical tips to help you find the perfect balance for your projects.

Understanding the Speed vs. Quality Tradeoff

Before diving into specific settings, it's important to understand why print speed affects quality. When your printer moves faster:

  • The printer's components experience more vibration and momentum shifts
  • The extruder has less time to precisely lay down filament
  • Layer cooling may be insufficient before the next layer is deposited
  • Corners and detailed features have less precision due to inertia

However, this doesn't mean you need to print everything at a snail's pace to get good results. Different parts of the print can benefit from different speeds, and many other settings can be optimized to maintain quality even at higher speeds.

Key Factors Affecting Print Quality and Speed

Layer Height

Layer height is perhaps the most significant factor affecting both print time and quality:

  • Thinner layers (0.1mm or less): Smoother surface finish, better detail reproduction, but significantly longer print times
  • Thicker layers (0.2mm to 0.3mm): Faster prints, but more visible layer lines and less detail
  • Very thick layers (0.3mm+): Very fast prints, but rough appearance and potential structural issues

For many functional parts, a layer height of 0.2mm provides a good balance. For display models or parts with fine details, 0.12mm or 0.16mm may be better despite the longer print time.

Print Speed Settings

Most slicers allow you to set different speeds for different parts of your print:

  • Outer walls: Printing these slower (25-40mm/s) dramatically improves surface quality
  • Inner walls: Can be printed faster (40-60mm/s) as they're not visible
  • Infill: Can be printed much faster (60-100mm/s) as internal quality is less critical
  • Top/bottom layers: Moderate speeds (30-50mm/s) help ensure good flat surfaces
  • Supports: Can be printed very fast (80-120mm/s) as their appearance doesn't matter

By setting appropriate speeds for each component, you can significantly reduce print time while maintaining quality where it counts.

Temperature

Optimal temperature settings are crucial when increasing print speed:

  • Higher temperatures allow filament to flow more quickly through the nozzle
  • For faster prints, increase temperature by 5-10°C above your normal settings
  • Be careful not to go too hot, as this can cause stringing, oozing, and other issues

Cooling

Proper cooling becomes even more important at higher speeds:

  • Ensure your part cooling fan is running at optimal speed (usually 100% for PLA, less for other materials)
  • Consider adding auxiliary cooling for very fast prints
  • Minimum layer time settings can help ensure adequate cooling on small layers

Acceleration and Jerk Settings

These advanced settings control how quickly your printer changes direction:

  • Lower acceleration values (500-1000mm/s²) produce higher quality but slower prints
  • Higher acceleration values (1500-3000mm/s²) speed up prints but may introduce ringing artifacts
  • Jerk (or junction deviation) controls how sharply the printer can change direction

For most consumer printers, keeping acceleration between 500-1500mm/s² provides a good balance.

Nozzle Size

The diameter of your nozzle has a significant impact:

  • Smaller nozzles (0.2-0.3mm): Better detail, but much slower printing
  • Standard nozzles (0.4mm): Good all-around balance
  • Larger nozzles (0.6-1.0mm): Much faster printing, but less detail and precision

For functional parts where speed matters more than aesthetics, upgrading to a 0.6mm nozzle can dramatically reduce print times while maintaining adequate quality.

Practical Strategies for Optimizing Speed and Quality

1. Adaptive Layer Height

Some slicers now offer adaptive layer height, which automatically varies layer thickness based on the geometry of your model:

  • Flat, vertical sections use thicker layers for speed
  • Curved surfaces and details use thinner layers for quality
  • This can reduce print time by 20-30% while maintaining good detail where needed

2. Optimize Your Model Design

How you design your 3D model greatly affects the speed/quality balance:

  • Orient models to minimize overhangs and support material
  • Round corners where possible (the printer can navigate them faster)
  • Consider splitting large models into parts that can be printed optimally
  • Remove unnecessary details from functional parts

3. Use Different Profiles for Different Needs

Create several slicer profiles tailored to different situations:

  • Draft quality: Fast prints for prototyping and testing fit (0.28-0.32mm layers, faster speeds)
  • Standard quality: Good balance for most functional parts (0.2mm layers, moderate speeds)
  • High quality: For display models and fine details (0.12mm layers, slower outer wall speed)

4. Hardware Considerations

Your printer's hardware capabilities set the upper limit for speed:

  • Direct drive extruders can generally handle faster retractions than Bowden setups
  • Rigid frames allow higher speeds without quality loss
  • Better cooling systems support faster printing
  • Consider upgrades that target your specific bottlenecks

Finding Your Printer's Sweet Spot

Every printer has its own "sweet spot" where it delivers the best balance of speed and quality. To find yours:

  1. Print a calibration tower: Many are available online that vary speed as they print upward
  2. Examine the results: Look for the highest speed that still produces acceptable quality
  3. Test specific features: Print small models with overhangs, bridges, and fine details at different speeds
  4. Document your findings: Create profiles based on what works best for your printer

Materials Matter: Speed vs. Quality for Different Filaments

PLA

PLA is forgiving and can be printed relatively quickly:

  • Can often be printed at 60-80mm/s for general features
  • Requires good cooling, especially at higher speeds
  • Higher temperatures (205-215°C) help with faster extrusion

PETG

PETG benefits from slightly slower speeds:

  • 40-60mm/s works well for most features
  • Needs less cooling than PLA, especially for layer adhesion
  • Higher temperatures (235-250°C) are essential for good flow

ABS/ASA

These materials are more challenging at high speeds:

  • 40-60mm/s is typical for good results
  • Enclosure becomes more important at higher speeds to prevent warping
  • Minimal cooling is preferred for layer adhesion

TPU and Flexible Filaments

Flexible filaments require much slower speeds:

  • 15-30mm/s is recommended for most flexible materials
  • Direct drive extruders perform much better at higher speeds with flexibles
  • Pushing speed too high leads to extrusion issues and failed prints

The Business Perspective: When to Prioritize Speed or Quality

If you're using 3D printing professionally, consider these factors:

When to Prioritize Speed:

  • For rapid prototyping and iterative design
  • When printing multiple parts for production
  • For internal components not visible in the final product
  • When time constraints are your primary concern

When to Prioritize Quality:

  • Client-facing presentation models
  • Final products for sale
  • Parts requiring precise tolerances
  • When appearance is a key factor

Conclusion: Your Perfect Balance

Finding the ideal balance between print speed and quality is a continuous process of experimentation and refinement. Start with these guidelines, but don't be afraid to test and adjust settings for your specific printer and project needs.

The good news is that with modern slicers and printers, you can often achieve both good speed and quality by being strategic about where you make compromises. By using variable settings for different parts of your print, you can prioritize quality where it matters most while still keeping overall print times reasonable.

Remember that the "perfect" balance depends on your specific requirements. A prototype might benefit from speed over quality, while a display piece deserves the extra time for higher quality. With the knowledge from this guide, you'll be equipped to make informed decisions about the tradeoffs that make the most sense for each project.
